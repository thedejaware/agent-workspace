# Viability Assessment Criteria

## Detailed Scoring Rubric (0-100 Points)

### Overview

This framework provides a quantitative method for assessing idea viability across 5 dimensions. Each dimension is scored 0-20 points, totaling 100 points maximum.

**Viability Levels:**
- **70-100 points:** HIGH VIABILITY - Strong fundamentals, worth building
- **40-69 points:** MEDIUM VIABILITY - Has potential but needs refinement
- **0-39 points:** LOW VIABILITY - Fundamental issues, likely to fail

**Confidence Levels:**
- **HIGH (70-100%):** Multiple independent sources, substantial data
- **MEDIUM (40-69%):** Limited sources, some extrapolation
- **LOW (<40%):** Speculative, needs more research

## Dimension 1: Market Size & Growth (0-20 points)

### Scoring Criteria

**18-20 points: Excellent Market Opportunity**
- TAM ‚â• $100M with clear path to capture share
- Growth rate ‚â• 20% annually (fast growing)
- Strong macro trends supporting market expansion
- Multiple credible sources confirm market size
- Market is growing and will continue to grow

**Example:** "Remote work collaboration tools - $5B TAM growing 25% annually due to permanent remote work trend"

**15-17 points: Strong Market Opportunity**
- TAM $20-100M, realistic addressable portion
- Growth rate 10-20% annually (steady growth)
- Positive macro trends supporting market
- Good sources confirm market size
- Market is healthy and expanding

**Example:** "Project management for architecture firms - $50M TAM growing 12% annually"

**10-14 points: Moderate Market Opportunity**
- TAM $5-20M, smaller but viable market
- Growth rate 5-10% annually (slow growth)
- Neutral or mixed macro trends
- Limited sources, some extrapolation needed
- Market is stable but not exciting

**Example:** "Time tracking for freelance writers - $8M TAM growing 6% annually"

**5-9 points: Small Market Opportunity**
- TAM <$5M, very small market
- Growth rate 0-5% annually (flat)
- Some negative macro trends
- Weak sources, heavy extrapolation
- Market is stable but limited upside

**Example:** "Invoicing for underwater basket weavers - $2M TAM growing 2% annually"

**0-4 points: Insufficient Market**
- No meaningful TAM or TAM <$1M
- Declining market (negative growth)
- Strong negative macro trends
- No credible sources confirm market
- Market is dying or non-existent

**Example:** "Fax machine management software - declining 15% annually"

### Key Questions to Answer

1. **Is the TAM substantial?**
   - Can support multiple players making $1M+ ARR?
   - Large enough to be interesting even with small market share?

2. **Is the market growing?**
   - What's the CAGR over past 3 years?
   - What's the projected CAGR for next 3-5 years?

3. **What macro trends affect this market?**
   - Technology enablers (AI, mobile, cloud)?
   - Regulatory changes (GDPR, SOC2)?
   - Societal shifts (remote work, gig economy)?
   - Economic factors (recession-proof vs cyclical)?

4. **How reliable is the market data?**
   - Multiple independent sources?
   - Recent data (<2 years old)?
   - Clear methodology?

### Red Flags (Reduce Score)

- ‚ùå Only one source for market size
- ‚ùå Market size based on "if 1% of world population..."
- ‚ùå Market declining >5% annually
- ‚ùå All data is >3 years old
- ‚ùå TAM is really just one big company's revenue

### Green Flags (Increase Score)

- ‚úÖ 3+ credible sources align on market size
- ‚úÖ Clear market growth driven by measurable trends
- ‚úÖ Recent analyst reports from Gartner/Forrester/IDC
- ‚úÖ Proven willingness to pay (existing solutions have revenue)
- ‚úÖ Market is undergoing transformation (new technology, new regulations)

## Dimension 2: Competitive Advantage (0-20 points)

### Scoring Criteria

**18-20 points: Strong, Defensible Advantage**
- Clear, unique differentiation that's hard to replicate
- Strong moat (network effects, data, integrations, brand)
- Competitors are weak, unfocused, or in different segments
- 0-2 direct competitors in your specific niche
- Structural advantage (vertical focus, unique capability)

**Example:** "Only invoice tool that integrates natively with construction project management software used by 80% of contractors"

**15-17 points: Good Differentiation**
- Good differentiation with some barriers to entry
- Moderate moat (integrations, domain expertise)
- 3-5 direct competitors, but room for multiple winners
- Can compete on execution and focus
- Differentiation is meaningful to customers

**Example:** "Project management built specifically for creative agencies, while competitors target all industries"

**10-14 points: Some Differentiation**
- Some differentiation but easily copyable
- Weak moat (mostly execution-dependent)
- 5-10 direct competitors, crowded but manageable
- Must execute better than competitors
- Differentiation is incremental, not revolutionary

**Example:** "Time tracker with better mobile app UX than competitors"

**5-9 points: Weak Differentiation**
- Minimal meaningful differentiation
- No moat, purely execution-based competition
- 10-20 direct competitors, market is crowded
- Commoditized market with price pressure
- Differentiation is subjective or unproven

**Example:** "Another to-do list app with 'cleaner design'"

**0-4 points: No Differentiation**
- No meaningful differentiation from competitors
- Market dominated by strong incumbents
- 20+ direct competitors or one dominant player (>50% share)
- Impossible to compete without massive capital
- Copying existing solutions without improvement

**Example:** "A CRM like Salesforce but for small businesses" (HubSpot, Pipedrive, etc. already dominate)

### Key Questions to Answer

1. **What makes you different?**
   - Is it structural (vertical, use case) or subjective (better UX)?
   - Can competitors easily copy this?
   - Is it meaningful to customers (would they switch for this)?

2. **Who are your direct competitors?**
   - How many are there?
   - How well-funded are they?
   - How entrenched are they (users, revenue, brand)?

3. **Do you have a moat?**
   - Network effects? (More users = better product)
   - Data moat? (Proprietary data improves product)
   - Integration moat? (Deep integrations create switching costs)
   - Brand moat? (Category ownership)
   - Technology moat? (Proprietary tech, patents)

4. **Can you realistically compete?**
   - Against established, funded competitors?
   - Without their resources (team, capital, brand)?
   - In a reasonable timeframe (<18 months to meaningful traction)?

### Red Flags (Reduce Score)

- ‚ùå 10+ well-funded direct competitors
- ‚ùå Your only differentiation is "better UX" or "we'll execute better"
- ‚ùå Dominant player has >50% market share and strong moat
- ‚ùå Competitors have raised recent large rounds for your exact idea
- ‚ùå You're entering a race to the bottom (price competition)

### Green Flags (Increase Score)

- ‚úÖ 0-3 direct competitors in your specific niche
- ‚úÖ Structural differentiation (vertical, integration, model)
- ‚úÖ Existing competitors are succeeding (proven market)
- ‚úÖ Clear moat that compounds over time
- ‚úÖ Competitors focused on different segments/use cases

## Dimension 3: Customer Demand Evidence (0-20 points)

### Scoring Criteria

**18-20 points: Strong, Validated Demand**
- Extensive evidence of customers actively seeking solutions
- Many people discussing pain point across multiple platforms
- Clear evidence of willingness to pay (reviews mention price)
- Pain point is frequent, urgent, and expensive to not solve
- Existing solutions have strong revenue/traction

**Example:** "Reddit threads with 500+ upvotes begging for this tool, 10+ blog posts about the problem, existing solutions charging $50/month with positive reviews"

**15-17 points: Good Demand Signals**
- Good evidence of demand across multiple sources
- Regular discussions about the problem
- Some evidence of willingness to pay
- Pain point is frequent and meaningful
- Existing solutions have moderate traction

**Example:** "Multiple Reddit discussions, Twitter complaints, existing solutions with dozens of paying customers"

**10-14 points: Moderate Demand Signals**
- Some evidence of demand but limited
- Occasional discussions about the problem
- Unclear willingness to pay
- Pain point exists but may not be urgent
- Existing solutions have some users

**Example:** "Few Reddit threads, some Twitter mentions, existing solutions but unclear traction"

**5-9 points: Weak Demand Signals**
- Minimal evidence of demand
- Very little discussion about the problem
- No clear evidence of willingness to pay
- Pain point is minor or infrequent
- Existing solutions have few users

**Example:** "One blog post about the problem, no social media discussion, existing solutions appear inactive"

**0-4 points: No Demand Evidence**
- No evidence of demand found
- No one discussing the problem
- No existing solutions (red flag - may not be a real problem)
- Pain point appears to be assumption, not reality
- "Solution looking for a problem"

**Example:** "No Reddit threads, no Twitter mentions, no existing solutions, no evidence anyone wants this"

### Key Questions to Answer

1. **Are people actively complaining about this problem?**
   - On Reddit? (Search relevant subreddits)
   - On X/Twitter? (Search problem keywords)
   - On LinkedIn? (Professional discussions)
   - In forums/communities? (Niche discussion boards)

2. **Are people paying for solutions?**
   - Do existing solutions have paying customers?
   - What are they paying? ($10/month vs $100/month)
   - Are reviews positive about value for money?

3. **How severe is the pain point?**
   - Is it mentioned frequently?
   - Do people say "I desperately need this" or "would be nice to have"?
   - Are they actively seeking solutions or just venting?

4. **What are current workarounds?**
   - What do people do today without your solution?
   - How painful is the workaround?
   - Are they willing to leave workaround for paid solution?

### Research Sources to Check

**Reddit:**
- Search relevant subreddits for problem keywords
- Look for high-upvote posts about the pain point
- Check if people are asking for tool recommendations
- Read comments for severity of pain

**X/Twitter:**
- Search "[problem] frustrated"
- Search "[current solution] sucks"
- Look for people asking for alternatives
- Check sentiment and frequency

**LinkedIn:**
- Search for professional discussions about problem
- Check relevant group discussions
- Look for articles about the problem

**Review Sites:**
- G2, Capterra, Trustpilot for existing solutions
- What do customers love/hate?
- What features are they requesting?
- Are they happy with pricing?

**Forums & Communities:**
- Niche forums related to target customer
- Discord/Slack communities
- Hacker News, Indie Hackers for tech products
- Industry-specific forums

### Red Flags (Reduce Score)

- ‚ùå Zero discussion about the problem across all platforms
- ‚ùå People say "nice to have" not "must have"
- ‚ùå No existing solutions (may not be a real problem)
- ‚ùå Complaints are very rare (problem is infrequent)
- ‚ùå Current workarounds seem acceptable

### Green Flags (Increase Score)

- ‚úÖ Multiple high-engagement discussions across platforms
- ‚úÖ People using phrases like "desperately need" or "would pay for"
- ‚úÖ Existing solutions have paying customers despite flaws
- ‚úÖ Pain point mentioned frequently and recently
- ‚úÖ Current workarounds are clearly painful/time-consuming

## Dimension 4: Execution Feasibility (0-20 points)

### Scoring Criteria

**18-20 points: Very Feasible (<1 Week MVP)**
- Core functionality can be built in <1 week
- Standard tech stack (React, Node, Postgres, etc.)
- No complex integrations or APIs required
- No specialized expertise needed
- Clear, simple MVP path

**Example:** "Landing page + Stripe payment + email automation with existing tools"

**15-17 points: Feasible (1-2 Weeks MVP)**
- Core functionality can be built in 1-2 weeks
- Standard tech stack with common patterns
- 1-2 API integrations, all well-documented
- General software development skills sufficient
- Straightforward MVP, some complexity

**Example:** "CRUD app with Stripe billing, OAuth login, and email notifications"

**10-14 points: Moderate Complexity (2-4 Weeks)**
- MVP requires 2-4 weeks of focused development
- Some specialized tech or frameworks
- 3-5 API integrations, varying documentation quality
- May require learning new technologies
- MVP is achievable but requires effort

**Example:** "Real-time collaboration features, webhook processing, and third-party API integration"

**5-9 points: High Complexity (1-2 Months)**
- MVP requires 1-2 months of development
- Specialized tech stack or custom algorithms
- Complex integrations or data processing
- Requires specialized expertise (ML, distributed systems)
- MVP is challenging for solopreneur

**Example:** "Custom LLM fine-tuning, real-time video processing, complex data pipelines"

**0-4 points: Very High Complexity (>2 Months)**
- MVP requires >2 months of development
- Highly specialized technology
- Very complex integrations or infrastructure
- Requires team with specialized skills
- Not feasible for solopreneur

**Example:** "Enterprise-grade distributed system, custom AI models, hardware integration"

### Key Questions to Answer

1. **What's the core functionality for MVP?**
   - What's the absolute minimum to test the value proposition?
   - Can you cut scope dramatically for v1?

2. **What's the tech stack?**
   - Standard technologies or specialized?
   - Frameworks you already know?
   - Infrastructure complexity (simple hosting vs complex setup)?

3. **What are the critical integrations?**
   - Which external APIs are required?
   - Are they well-documented?
   - Are they available/affordable?
   - Do they have rate limits or restrictions?

4. **What's the riskiest dependency?**
   - What could block you completely?
   - What requires specialized knowledge you don't have?
   - What could take much longer than expected?

5. **Can you realistically build this?**
   - With your current skills?
   - In the proposed timeframe?
   - As a solopreneur or do you need a team?

### Complexity Factors

**Increases Complexity:**
- Real-time features (WebSockets, live updates)
- Video/audio processing
- Machine learning / AI features
- Complex algorithms (recommendation engines, matching)
- Distributed systems
- Mobile apps (iOS + Android)
- Browser extensions
- Hardware integrations
- Compliance requirements (HIPAA, SOC2)
- Multi-tenancy / white-labeling

**Decreases Complexity:**
- Standard CRUD operations
- Server-side rendered web app
- Using no-code/low-code tools where possible
- Leveraging existing APIs (vs building from scratch)
- Focusing on single platform (web OR mobile, not both)
- Simple user flows
- Using battle-tested frameworks

### Red Flags (Reduce Score)

- ‚ùå Requires technologies you've never used
- ‚ùå Critical dependency on complex/undocumented API
- ‚ùå Needs specialized expertise (ML, blockchain, etc.)
- ‚ùå Infrastructure is complex (distributed, real-time, high-scale)
- ‚ùå Scope creep likely (hard to define minimal MVP)

### Green Flags (Increase Score)

- ‚úÖ Using tech stack you already know
- ‚úÖ Simple, well-defined MVP
- ‚úÖ All integrations are well-documented and available
- ‚úÖ Can leverage existing tools/libraries
- ‚úÖ Similar products exist (proven it's buildable)

## Dimension 5: Revenue Potential (0-20 points)

### Scoring Criteria

**18-20 points: Strong Revenue Potential (‚â•$10k MRR Year 1)**
- Clear path to $10k+ MRR in Year 1
- Proven pricing model in market ($50-500/month)
- Strong unit economics (LTV/CAC >3x)
- Low customer acquisition cost (<$100)
- High customer lifetime value (>$1000)
- Quick payback period (<6 months)

**Example:** "B2B SaaS selling at $99/month, CAC $50, 200 customers in year 1 = $19.8k MRR"

**15-17 points: Good Revenue Potential ($5-10k MRR Year 1)**
- Realistic path to $5-10k MRR in Year 1
- Validated pricing model ($20-100/month)
- Good unit economics (LTV/CAC 2-3x)
- Moderate CAC ($100-200)
- Moderate LTV ($500-1000)
- Reasonable payback period (6-12 months)

**Example:** "B2C SaaS at $29/month, CAC $80, 250 customers in year 1 = $7.25k MRR"

**10-14 points: Moderate Revenue Potential ($2-5k MRR Year 1)**
- Possible to reach $2-5k MRR in Year 1
- Uncertain pricing model ($10-30/month)
- Moderate unit economics (LTV/CAC 1-2x)
- Higher CAC ($200-500)
- Lower LTV ($200-500)
- Long payback period (12-18 months)

**Example:** "Consumer app at $9.99/month, CAC $30, 300 customers in year 1 = $2.99k MRR"

**5-9 points: Low Revenue Potential (<$2k MRR Year 1)**
- Challenging to reach $2k MRR in Year 1
- Unclear pricing model (<$10/month or unproven)
- Weak unit economics (LTV/CAC <1x)
- High CAC (>$500)
- Low LTV (<$200)
- Very long payback period (>18 months)

**Example:** "Free tool with unclear monetization path"

**0-4 points: No Clear Revenue Path**
- No clear path to meaningful revenue
- No one paying for similar solutions
- Broken unit economics (CAC > LTV)
- Customers unwilling to pay
- Market expects free solutions

**Example:** "Social network expecting ads to monetize eventually"

### Key Questions to Answer

1. **What will you charge?**
   - Based on competitor pricing research
   - What are customers paying for similar value?
   - Subscription vs one-time vs usage-based?

2. **How many paying customers in Year 1?**
   - Conservative estimate (10th percentile)
   - Realistic estimate (50th percentile)
   - Optimistic estimate (90th percentile)

3. **What's the customer acquisition cost?**
   - Organic (content, SEO): $20-100
   - Paid ads: $50-500+
   - Sales-led: $500-5000+
   - Based on competitor channel analysis

4. **What's the customer lifetime value?**
   - Monthly price √ó months retained
   - What's typical churn in this category?
   - Any expansion revenue (upgrades, upsells)?

5. **Do the unit economics work?**
   - LTV must be >3x CAC for healthy business
   - Payback period should be <12 months
   - Path to profitability clear

- ‚úÖ Usage-based pricing (aligns with value)

## Confidence Level Calibration

### HIGH Confidence (70-100%)

**Criteria:**
- 3+ independent, authoritative sources
- Recent data (<12 months old)
- Quantitative metrics available
- Clear patterns across all sources
- Direct evidence (not extrapolated)

**When to assign:**
- Market size from multiple analyst reports
- Competitor pricing from public websites
- Customer demand from active communities (1000+ members)
- Revenue projections based on competitor benchmarks

**Example:** "Market size: $500M (Gartner 2025), $480M (Forrester 2025), $520M (IDC 2026). Confidence: HIGH (95%)"

### MEDIUM Confidence (40-69%)

**Criteria:**
- 1-2 reasonably authoritative sources
- Somewhat dated data (1-2 years old)
- Mix of quantitative and qualitative
- Some extrapolation required
- Adjacent market used as proxy

**When to assign:**
- Market size estimated from adjacent markets
- Competitor traction from indirect signals (job postings, traffic)
- Customer demand from smaller communities (100-1000 members)
- Revenue projections with several assumptions

**Example:** "Market size: ~$50M estimated from adjacent $500M market √ó 10% segment. Confidence: MEDIUM (60%)"

### LOW Confidence (<40%)

**Criteria:**
- 0-1 sources, or unreliable sources
- Outdated data (>2 years old)
- Heavy extrapolation and assumptions
- Mostly qualitative/anecdotal
- Contradictory signals

**When to assign:**
- Market size calculated from first principles
- Competitor status unknown (no public data)
- Customer demand assumed based on logic
- Revenue projections highly speculative

**Example:** "Market size: ~$10M based on [target customers] √ó [assumed spend]. Confidence: LOW (30%). Requires validation."

**Action when confidence is LOW:**
- Flag prominently in assessment
- Recommend user validates independently
- Suggest specific research to increase confidence
- Be extra conservative in viability score

## Decision Framework

### When Scores Conflict

**Scenario 1: High market, low feasibility**
- Example: 18/20 market, 5/20 feasibility
- Recommendation: Consider partnering, hiring, or simplifying scope
- Could be MEDIUM viability if you can address feasibility

**Scenario 2: Low market, high everything else**
- Example: 6/20 market, 18/20 on all others
- Recommendation: TRASH IT - no matter how well executed, market limits success
- Market size is often the binding constraint

**Scenario 3: High demand, high competition**
- Example: 18/20 demand, 6/20 competition
- Recommendation: MEDIUM - proven market but hard to win
- Need exceptional execution or different angle

**Scenario 4: Low demand evidence, everything else good**
- Example: 5/20 demand, 15+ on all others
- Recommendation: HIGH RISK - may not be a real problem
- Recommend extensive customer validation before building

### Adjustment Factors

**Increase viability score if:**
- You have unfair advantage (domain expertise, distribution, capital)
- You've already validated with customers (pre-sales, waitlist)
- You have unique insight others don't see yet
- Timing is exceptional (new regulation, new platform)

**Decrease viability score if:**
- You're unfamiliar with target customer
- You have no distribution advantage
- You're bootstrapped competing with funded companies
- Timing is wrong (too early or too late)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Be objective** - Score based on evidence, not optimism
2. **Market size matters most** - Can't succeed in tiny/declining markets
3. **Differentiation is critical** - "Better execution" isn't enough
4. **Demand must be validated** - Assumptions about demand are dangerous
5. **Feasibility gates execution** - Can't succeed if you can't build it
6. **Revenue must be realistic** - Fantasy projections lead to failure
7. **Confidence matters** - Low confidence = higher risk
8. **Context matters** - Adjust for unfair advantages or disadvantages
9. **Be honest** - Better to hear hard truth now than after building
10. **Iterate** - MEDIUM scores can become HIGH with refinement
